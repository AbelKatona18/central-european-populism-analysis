Presidents Áder and Schmitt, and your wives; Mr Speaker; leaders of the Hungarian diaspora; ladies and gentlemen;
As you are no doubt aware, a devastating earthquake struck Turkey and Syria nearly two weeks ago. The death toll stands at over 44,000, and sadly, this is not yet the end. Disaster strikes suddenly; it gives no warning, it does not knock, it simply bursts through the door. It is in times of trouble that we discover who we can count on. You can count on us, the Hungarians. 167 of our compatriots took part in the rescue efforts, both professionals and volunteers. Risking their own lives, they rescued 35 people from the rubble. Some of them are here with us today. Please allow us to welcome into our midst our heroes, who have honoured us with their presence. Ladies and gentlemen, thank you for your selfless work! A nation is proud of you! Please be so kind as to stand up, so that we may see you!
Ladies and gentlemen!
So much has happened over the past year – elections, war, the energy crisis, inflation – that I could actually speak for hours on end. Please, don’t start packing up; it’s too late now; if you’ve come, you’ve come. Tomorrow is Sunday, so you can even sit through a speech as long as those of Atatürk or Fidel Castro. Yet I shall keep it shorter, because during a long political lecture one can even lose one’s zest for life, and we have not gathered here to lose it, but precisely to regain our zest for life. And with that, I have plunged straight into the heart of my remarks. From the perspective of the future, the most important question today is whether the enormous changes taking place in European life – which present us with new intellectual, political, economic and military challenges – will take away or bring about the Hungarians’ zest for life. These changes are putting the whole of Hungarian life under pressure and posing new questions that strike at the very heart of our being. The success of the year 1923 depends on whether these questions inspire or discourage us.
Ladies and gentlemen!
I find myself caught up in international politics, and I am often reminded of that old Hungarian hit: ‘Mum, this isn’t the horse I wanted.’ And indeed, we Hungarians did not want to live in such a chaotic world. But as my mother would say: ‘My boy, this isn’t a request show.’ And she is right.
And yet things had been going so well; everything had been so carefully thought out. We had fought our way through the rubble left behind by the socialist governments ousted in 2010, the ruins of Eastern European socialism; we had overcome unemployment, the shrinking economy, foreign currency loans, the gloomy envy, the humiliation before the West, the sky-high utility bills, the bribes, the scheming to live off benefits, and the resigned indifference of ‘this is good enough’. We had just begun to believe that there would be a place in the sun for every Hungarian, and that that place would be here. It turned out that one can live better on a wage than on benefits, that a child is not a burden but a joy—or, to be precise, a burden in the sense that it is a joy. We had just begun to believe that there would still be grapes here, that there would still be soft bread for everyone. We realised that if we want to get by, we must not take from one another, because we know how to bake much larger loaves of bread than we have done so far. A million people have found work; never before have so many worked in Hungary. The Hungarian economy has tripled in size, and even the minimum wage is now higher than the average wage was under the Socialists. We have conjured up a national, Christian constitution worthy of us. With a courage that, if not death-defying, certainly defies Brussels, we have reorganised the Hungarian state, and, pushing aside the obstructionists, we have also built a new Hungarian economy in which everyone has been given the chance to find their own way. It was a gruelling ten years; we worked up a proper sweat, our knees and elbows were scraped raw, and we got quite a few blisters, but we felt it was worth it. We learnt how to thrive in a renewed Hungary; we saw that our efforts made sense, and it was already dawning on us that the Hungarian name would once again be a fine one, worthy of its former great renown. That is why, in the parliamentary elections, following the historic first two-thirds majority in 2010, we secured a two-thirds majority, and then three more two-thirds majorities. And we’ve secured it again now, even though the entire left has united against us, Brussels has tried to drain the treasury, and Gyuri and his lot have channelled four billion forints’ worth of dollars here from America so that their comrades would have something to shoot with – at us. They’ve come unstuck; they’ve miscalculated again. Not just a little, but a lot. And if I’m not mistaken, they’ll even end up paying for it.
Do you remember *Once Upon a Time in the West*? The dialogue at the start of the film? When Charles Bronson, the harmonica player, asks the three bandits waiting for him: “And Frank?” “Frank sent us.” “Did you bring me a horse?” “No.” To which the harmonica player replies: “That makes two too many.” This is what happened in Hungary in 2022. And if I’m not mistaken, the Hungarian Frank, our very own Ferink, is currently trying to round up the horses left without an owner. The lesson: when you look at your opponents, judge them not by their numbers, but by their abilities. It seems the Good Lord loves us.
Ladies and gentlemen!
Election victories aren’t usually just handed to you on a plate, especially not a two-thirds majority. There is hard work behind it, and the results of that work, which people appreciate. There is no victory otherwise, and a two-thirds majority certainly does not materialise. Of course, there are always naysayers who claim we were simply lucky. Fine, let it be luck – once. But four times? If you’re always lucky, it might be that you know something, such as loving your country and being prepared to fight for it. At home, if necessary, or out in the wider world, if necessary. The left needs to understand that millions of dollars and influential backers are not enough for victory. For victory, my dear friends, you need heart, and luck alone is not enough.
Ladies and Gentlemen!
Just when we thought we were finally back on our feet, in the spring of 2020 – three years ago now – COVID struck. It was painful, with irreplaceable losses. But we were right to hope that we would get through it, get back on our feet, and pick up where we left off. I thought we would soon arrive at the place we had always longed for. We would take our rightful place in the world, a place to which our talent, our diligence and our history – yes, our history – entitle us. We would be among the best, somewhere in the vanguard. Once again there will be many children, millions of hearts awaiting the good news: a well-ordered, beautiful and safe country, a green Carpathian Basin that survives climate change. And although the lion and the lamb will never live together, we hoped that the left would finally understand that this is a shared homeland, and we have no other.
And then war struck, or rather, broke out. It has been a year now, and by all human reckoning, it seems this could go on for a long time, perhaps even several years. Everything has changed. In politics and in the economy alike. The world has decidedly set its course towards the Wild West. From the COVID years, the world has not returned to the right path, but we have stepped into the years of war. In fact, since March 2020 – that is, for nearly three years now – we have been living our lives under constant pressure. And this could easily turn into four, or even five years. 2022 was the hardest of the 32 years since the change of regime. The hardest year.
When the West entered the war with sanctions, we had to rethink everything. This dominated the months following the April election. We had to rethink economic policy, defence and military policy, and our entire foreign policy. In the glare of the war, we had to re-examine all the major goals we had set ourselves in 2010, after securing our first two-thirds majority. We are slowly coming to the end of this work. As I see it, we need neither to abandon nor give up on these goals; we simply need to change the means. Foreign policy remains: we still want to make friends, not enemies. We want everyone – East and West, North and South – to have a stake in the success of the Hungarian people. Connection rather than bloc formation. And national unity remains a priority; Hungarians living abroad can continue to count on us, for we are of the same blood. Family policy remains. The work-based economy remains. The agreement with pensioners remains, and with it the thirteenth-month pension, and protection against utility price rises remains too. We will continue to link universities and the economy. We can keep the strategic sectors that have come under Hungarian control – the banking sector, the energy sector and the media industry – in Hungarian hands; indeed, we will also re-Hungarianise the telecommunications and information and communications technology sectors. And we won’t stop there; the windbag is already fluttering in the breeze. Sorry! And the promise made to the countryside will also be kept; we will launch unprecedented development projects, providing more money than the Hungarian countryside has ever seen, not even during the Austro-Hungarian Monarchy. Alongside productive agriculture, we will build up the processing sector; we will revive the Hungarian food industry, which was ruined by privatisation; we will have national champions in the food industry who will hold their own on the world market. Yet it is not right that Hungarians should have to buy food dumped on us from abroad. And there is also the plan to help the eastern part of the country catch up. It is time to finally unite Hunnia and Pannonia, both economically and in terms of living standards. That is why we are building bridges over the Danube; we are about to finish the Paks bridge and will soon begin work on the Mohács bridge. We will add the Debrecen–Nyíregyháza–Miskolc triangle to the Győr–Szombathely–Veszprém industrial zone. This requires energy, a great deal of energy – more than Hungary has ever needed before. That is why we will build power stations and transmission networks even if Brussels refuses to play a part in this. There will be other ways. And we will not give up on our most ambitious plan either: to ensure that the financial situation of families with children is better than that of those who do not have children. That is why, war or no war, we will continue to make new decisions to support families every year. Just as this year, women who have children will not pay personal income tax until they reach the age of thirty. That is what will happen. But I know that for us Hungarians, this is not enough. We are familiar with the joke left over from socialism: we know what will happen; the question is, what will happen in the meantime?
My dear friends!
If 2022 was the hardest year, then 2023 will be the most dangerous year since the change of regime. Alongside migration, which is slowly becoming a permanent feature, two new enemies, two new threats, are lurking. One is war, the other is inflation. If we want to return to the upward trajectory from which the COVID pandemic has pushed us off, then we must ward off these two threats, we must overcome them, we must fight our way through them. But how? That is what I am going to talk to you about today.
How can we overcome the threat of war? Deep down, we would simply put an end to it, but we do not have the strength to do so; this is not our league. Therefore, if we want to protect Hungary, if we want a peaceful life for ourselves, we have only one choice: we must stay out of the Russian–Ukrainian war. This has not been easy so far, and it will not be easy from now on either, because we are part of the Western world, members of NATO and the European Union, and everyone else there is on the side of war, or at least pretends to be. Can Hungary, under these circumstances, remain on the side of peace in direct opposition to our allies? Of course we can, for Hungary is an independent, free and sovereign state, and apart from God, we recognise no one above us. But is it right, is it morally sound, for us to stay out of the war? I am convinced that it is right; indeed, it is the only right thing to do. Russia has attacked Ukraine, so we must let Ukrainian refugees in, and we have done well to support them with the largest humanitarian aid operation in our country’s history. This is a commandment of basic humanity, and we are complying with it. But we also see that in the war in Ukraine, it is not the armies of good and evil that are fighting, but the forces of two Slavic countries engaged in a war that is limited in time and – for the time being – in space. This is their war, not ours. Hungary recognises Ukraine’s right to self-defence, to fight against external aggression, but it would not be right from any perspective, including a moral one, to place Ukraine’s interests before Hungary’s. The left in Hungary, too, is on the side of the war: it would supply weapons, shoulder the financial burden of the war, and sever ties with Russia. We will not do this. We will not supply weapons. We are also cautious with our money, because in the end, the money that is rightfully ours will be handed over to Ukraine by Brussels. Providing humanitarian aid to Ukraine does not mean severing our ties with Russia, because that would be contrary to our national interests, which we have the right to define for ourselves. That is why we will not consent to the introduction of sanctions on the gas, oil or nuclear industries, which would ruin Hungary. We know from the national consultation that there is national unity on this issue. We therefore maintain our economic relations with Russia, and indeed advise the entire Western world to do the same, because without relations there will be neither a ceasefire nor peace talks. That is why we do not consent to priests and church leaders being placed on sanctions lists; it is bad enough that this has happened to artists and sportspeople. And it is also important that we do not narrow our vision, that we do not become provincial. Let us look beyond Brussels. Every country outside Europe is aware of the limited significance of the war in Ukraine and the primacy of its own national interests. Let us not isolate ourselves from the level-headed part of the world. The Hungarian perspective is an exception only in Europe; it is the norm worldwide. The Hungarian government does not consider it realistic to assume that Russia poses a threat to the security of Hungary or Europe. This is true at most of nuclear weapons, but the war in Ukraine does not reduce the risk of their use; rather, it increases it. As far as conventional warfare is concerned, the war in Ukraine has demonstrated precisely that Russia would stand no chance against NATO. We understand that the Ukrainians are trying to convince Europe that the Russians will not stop at the Atlantic Ocean, but the Hungarians do not buy into this threat. The whole world has seen that the Russian armed forces are not in a position to attack NATO, nor will they be for a long time to come. I would remind you that Hungary proposed the establishment of a joint European force a decade ago, and today we can see what a pity it is that this fell on deaf ears at the time.
My dear friends!
Whilst the differences between our pro-peace stance and the pro-war stance of others are coming to the fore, the fact that we are in complete agreement regarding strategic objectives is being obscured. We want Russia not to pose a threat to Europe, and for there to be a sufficiently wide and deep buffer zone between Russia and Hungary: a sovereign Ukraine. The difference lies in the means; according to the warmongers, this can be achieved by defeating Russia, whereas we believe it can be achieved through an immediate ceasefire and negotiations. There is another compelling argument in favour of our proposal. Lives can only be saved by a ceasefire. The death toll is already in the hundreds of thousands; the pain, the bereavement, the rising number of orphans, and the waves of suffering can only be calmed by a ceasefire.
Ladies and Gentlemen!
The war has also brought to light some instructive and grave truths. Let us not pass them by without comment. First and foremost is the question of our NATO membership. Let us make it clear that NATO membership is vital for Hungary. We are too far east, on the eastern edge of the Western world, to be able to do without it. Further inland, of course, it would be easier; following the example of Austria and Switzerland, we too could toy with the idea of neutrality, but history has not granted us that luxury. NATO is a defence alliance. A military, defence alliance formed so that we might defend one another. That is why we joined, and that is why I felt a sense of historical vindication when, with 45 years of Soviet occupation behind us, I was able to sign the accession treaty. It is just as important for us to understand clearly what NATO is not. NATO is not a war alliance. NATO is not a war coalition. NATO membership entails no obligations beyond collective defence, and member states cannot expect one another to attack a third country together for the sake of some common military objective. If some NATO members or a group of them wish to carry out military action beyond the territory of the member states, they must do so outside the framework of NATO. Those who wish to go, go; those who do not wish to go, do not go.
My dear friends!
However strong and powerful they may be, those who believe they can keep war under control, manage it, and dose it out step by step are overestimating their own strength and underestimating the risky nature of war. Those who make these mistakes usually live far removed from the devastating reality of frontline combat. But we live here, and the war is raging on the soil of our neighbouring country. The people of Brussels have not yet given their lives in this war, but Hungarians have. Whilst Hungarian symbols are being torn down in Mukachevo and Hungarian leaders are being removed from the heads of our schools, many are dying a heroic death on the front line. The Hungarian minority in Transcarpathia does not deserve this. More respect for Hungarians in Mukachevo, Kyiv, Brussels and Washington!
Ladies and Gentlemen!
Europe is on the brink of war. It is balancing on a fine line. In fact, it is already engaged in an indirect war with Russia. If you supply weapons, if you provide the satellite intelligence necessary for military operations, if you train the soldiers of one of the warring parties, if you fund the entire functioning of one side’s state apparatus whilst imposing sanctions on the other, then no matter what you say, you are at war – for the time being, an indirect war. The danger of being drawn in has become a permanent feature. It began with helmets, continued with the delivery of equipment unsuitable for taking human life, and now we’ve reached the stage of sending tanks; fighter planes are already on the agenda, and soon we’ll be hearing about so-called peacekeeping forces as well. Like sleepwalkers on the rooftops. We should also understand how the warmongers were gripped by this madness, and how they ended up on the rooftops? We understand our Polish and Baltic friends, despite all our differences of opinion. Their history explains a great deal. But what about the rest?
This need not have happened; or, to be more precise, it could have happened differently. We could have given a guarantee that we would not admit Ukraine to NATO, but we did the opposite and reaffirmed our earlier decision from 2008 that we would admit them. We could also have followed the solution we chose in 2008, when the Russian-Georgian war broke out and Russia occupied 20 per cent of Georgia’s territory. At that time, we decided not to let the conflict escalate, and under the leadership of President Sarkozy – a brilliant negotiator – the conflict was successfully contained and a ceasefire was secured. We could also have done what we did in 2014 under Angela Merkel’s leadership, when Russia attacked Ukraine and annexed the Crimean Peninsula. We could have chosen war, something like the current one, but back then, we in the West decided otherwise: negotiation instead of fighting, peace instead of war. I remember there were warmongers back then too, but there was also strong German and French leadership that was courageous and acted in time. Thus, war was averted, and the Minsk Agreement was established. A year ago, the West decided otherwise. When Russia launched its attack, the West did not isolate the conflict but elevated it to a pan-European level. It could have classified it as a local, regional war or a military conflict between two Slavic states, as Hungary had proposed. What happened is yet another argument against the Brussels superstate and in favour of strong nation states. When the Member States decided, there was peace; when the imperial centre decided, there was war.
Ladies and Gentlemen!
Looking to the future, it is also instructive to consider how we lost our peace-loving allies. A year ago, we were not alone in the peace camp. There were, for example, the Germans, who supplied no weapons, only helmets. In contrast, in a few weeks’ time, Leopard tanks will be rolling across Ukrainian soil eastwards, down towards the Russian border. Perhaps even the old maps are still around. The Germans turned alongside the others, or rather, the others turned alongside the Germans. And so the camp of peace dwindled away. It is hardly credible that the Germans would have switched sides of their own accord. Today they act as if they had always been there. Modern German style: they do not simply switch sides, but openly declare that they are jumping straight to the front. They are thorough people; when they do something, they do it properly. The other countries thought that if even the Germans could not resist such external pressure, they would hardly be able to. So they drifted from the camp of peace into the camp of war. That left just the two of us: Hungary and the Vatican. We cannot complain about the company, but we must reckon with serious consequences.
We must face up to the fact that the war is becoming increasingly brutal and ruthless, so we would do well to prepare ourselves for the tone directed at us to become ever harsher and more merciless. Provocations, insults, threats and blackmail. I cannot promise that it will be easy, but I can promise that we will stand our ground. We are long past the era of diplomatic pressure that respected sovereignty. Where are those good old days now, when in 2014 Hillary Clinton sent just a ‘good friend’ here to bring the Hungarians to their senses with anti-government protests and a few bans? We manoeuvred well back then; our plan worked, and the friendly rescue squad arrived in the form of Donald Trump – fortunately not here, but in Washington. Much water has flowed down the Potomac since then. It’s just as well that they’ve kept their sense of humour in the White House, and President Biden sent a ‘press man’ as ambassador instead of a good friend, to squeeze the Hungarians into the war camp at any cost, to squeeze a declaration of support out of us. That’s fine; humour can help friendship weather the storm in difficult times. But we shouldn’t go so far as to send someone called Puccini next time.
Ladies and gentlemen!
We reckon there will be another election in America in 2024, and our Republican friends are flexing their muscles in preparation for a comeback. I also expect democracy to demonstrate its strength in Europe as well; public opinion will become increasingly pro-peace, demanding a ceasefire, peace talks, and more common sense , and if necessary, will elect new governments. It won’t exactly be a walk in the park, but the smoother and more comfortable paths all lead to war.
Ladies and Gentlemen!
We harbour no illusions; we are neither naive, nor ‘68 flower children, nor dreamy pacifists. We know that the negotiations will not take place between the Ukrainians and the Russians. Peace will come when the Americans and the Russians negotiate with one another. This will inevitably happen, but the later it happens, the higher the price we will all pay for it. According to the warmongers, time is on the side of the Ukrainians and the West, so the fighting must continue; this will shift the balance of power, leading to victory over Russia, and victory will bring peace. But the Hungarian government believes that further fighting will not bring victory or peace, but rather the deaths of hundreds of thousands more, a widening conflict, countries drawn into open warfare, years of war, destruction, suffering and the threat of a world war. So let us Hungarians remain committed to peace, but let the Minister of Defence keep the gunpowder dry. So much for war.
Ladies and Gentlemen!
If we want to combat inflation, we must start with understanding. Why is there inflation across the whole of Europe? Brussels has brought this trouble upon us with sanctions imposed on energy sources. The name of the disease is sanctions-induced inflation, and the virus is the Brussels sanctions. Sanctions are the weapons of Brussels’s war policy. They were aimed at Russia, but they hit Europe. It was not so long ago that Brussels promised that these sanctions would bring an end to the war. A year has passed, and the end of the war has not come any closer; on the contrary, it is receding further into the distance. They also promised that the sanctions would not be extended to energy. Yet they did so anyway. The price of natural gas has risen many-fold, reaching the €350 mark at the end of August. This is a record; we have not seen anything like it in living memory. The situation has improved by now, but the price of natural gas is still several times higher than the €20 price of two years ago. What’s more – and few people know this – in Brussels they have linked the price of gas to the price of electricity. It was all in vain that the Poles and we protested. A rise in gas prices thus immediately leads to a rise in electricity prices, even if the electricity is generated not by gas turbines but by solar, wind, hydro, coal-fired power stations or nuclear energy. It is basic economics that soaring energy prices drive up the cost of every product. This is particularly true if you import most of your energy from abroad, as Hungary does. What’s more, it has turned out that we haven’t been siphoning off resources from Russia, but have actually been giving them more money. In 2022, profits in the global oil and gas industry rose by 70 per cent, even though the giants in question did not innovate in any way, did not produce more, but simply pocketed the extra profits from the sanctions, which were paid for by the people of Europe. The sanctions took 4,000 billion forints out of Hungarians’ pockets in 2022. Four thousand billion forints! That is how much more Hungarian companies, the state and families spent on energy alone in Hungary because of the sanctions. Companies could have spent this sum on pay rises, the state on tax cuts or family support, and families on buying a home or on their children.
One stands amongst the glass palaces of Brussels and simply cannot believe what is going on there. We must face the reality: instead of help, Brussels is delivering only further sanctions. The Brussels bureaucracy, with well-considered ill intent, did not give Hungary or Poland the share of the European Recovery Programme to which we were entitled. We did not receive any of the money in 2022 – the most difficult year – that the Member States borrowed as a joint loan, and we Hungarians will have to pay back our share. They are nitpicking at the rule of law in Hungary, whilst the ‘prison van’ maintains a constant watch outside the European Parliament building. In reality, it is the Member States that should be keeping Brussels in check, not the other way round. I hope this will be the case after the 2024 European elections. If Brussels is determined to wage war, then it should be waging war on inflation. It is not doing so. We, however, are constantly fighting our own battle. We have already introduced some two dozen measures to protect families and businesses.
And, my friends, the most important thing now is that we must not view inflation as an inevitable act of God. And although inflation is at its peak and places a heavy burden on families, we must not be frightened by it, we must not freeze, and we must not resign ourselves to it. We must take action, and it will bear fruit. I learnt from Sándor Demján that there are no fixed rules in times of crisis. We must intervene boldly in the economy. This is what we are doing, which is why an average family today saves 181,000 forints a month thanks to reduced utility prices. This is unique across Europe. The left is demanding the withdrawal of the food price cap, but it will remain in place until we can put inflation on a downward trajectory. The left is also demanding the abolition of the retail interest rate cap, unsurprisingly in tandem with the banks. But the interest rate cap protects 350,000 families from interest rate spikes, and until interest rates start to fall, the cap must remain in place. Instead of scrapping it, we have extended it to student loans. In this way, we are now protecting 200,000 students from inflation. The training loan is interest-free, and the interest rate on the discretionary student loan is half the market rate. And now we are also introducing the discounted county travel pass. From 1 May, we are offering monthly national and county travel passes valid for both buses and trains. The county travel pass will cost 9,450 forints, and the monthly national travel pass 18,900 forints. Those who use public transport to get to work can save a significant amount of money.
Ladies and Gentlemen!
Forging a good shield, one that can withstand even the heaviest blows, is a costly and expensive undertaking. That is why we must take the excess profits from where they arise. We have taxed the banks, the energy companies and the multinational corporations. And we are channelling the excess profits collected into the utility cost protection fund.
All in all, 2022 was a year that could have broken the backbone of the Hungarian economy. The official doomsayers, former central bank governors of renown, and right-wing economists who had switched sides had anticipated this, and were already busy burying us. Bankruptcies, unemployment, a collapse of the forint, insolvency, Armageddon. That was the stuff of left-wing prophecies. Now, in February, employment is higher than ever before, foreign exchange reserves are at record levels, and the forint has stabilised. The truth is that, alongside and despite painfully high inflation, the Hungarian economy broke three records at once in 2022. A hat-trick. I hope Captain Rossi is listening. Never before have so many people been in work in Hungary. Our exports set a record, and never has there been as much investment in Hungary as there was in 2022. That is why, despite high prices, we are still standing strong, and why the economy will not slow down in 2023 either. Inflation is like a tiger, and you only have one bullet. If you miss, it will devour you. Please trust us, we will hit the mark. You can even bet on it: by the end of the year, we will bring inflation down to single digits.
My dear friends!
As we can see, the situation is serious, but not hopeless. In fact, it is rather encouraging. Hungarians’ survival instincts are working; they have a clear grasp of the situation, and as the national consultation showed, there is broad agreement on the main objectives. I would like to take this opportunity to thank everyone who took part in the national consultation! We will stay out of the war; Hungary will remain an island of peace and security; and we will bring inflation under control – this is always the government’s job, and there will be no mistake in it. But there is something else here that even the most self-assured government cannot tackle alone. You know, everyone has heard about the disgraceful incident that took place in one of our schools. One simply cannot understand why the sky does not fall, why the earth does not open up to swallow up the person who belongs there.
My dear friends!
Let’s be blunt. There is no excuse for paedophilia. Children are sacred and inviolable. It is the duty of adults to protect children at all costs. And we don’t care that the world has gone mad. We don’t care what sort of repulsive fads some people indulge in. We don’t care what excuses Brussels comes up with to explain the inexplicable. This is Hungary! And this is where Europe’s strictest child protection system must be in place! The legislation is in place, and any gaps will be filled, but even the most determined government cannot succeed in this matter alone. Everyone will be needed here: parents, grandparents, mums and dads, teachers and carers. Because gender propaganda isn’t some daft nonsense, it isn’t just rainbow-coloured drivel, but the greatest danger lurking over our children! We want them to leave our children alone, because enough is enough! Such things have absolutely no place in Hungary, and certainly not in our schools. I am counting on you, we are counting on every right-thinking Hungarian, so that we can accomplish this task once and for all, together, in 2023!
God be with us all, Hungary above all! Come on, Hungary! Come on, Hungarians!
